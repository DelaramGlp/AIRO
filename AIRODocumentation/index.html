AIRO Ontology Specification

Abstract

Risk is a key concept in the draft AI Act, which is the European Commission proposal for AI regulation published in April 2021. The risk-based approach taken by the Act means it regulates development and use of AI systems according to the extent of risk and harm they impose. AI Act lays down obligations on identification, management and documentation of risks. Conforming to these obligations requires maintaining and querying information associated with AI risk management. AIRO is an ontology for expressing risk of AI systems based on the requirements of the AI Act and ISO 31000 series of standards. AIRO assists stakeholders in maintaining AI risk information, complying with regulations such as the AI Act, and performing impact assessments.

AIRO: Overview back to ToC

The AI Risk Ontology (AIRO) is an ontology for expressing information associated with high-risk AI systems based on the requirements of the proposed AI Act and ISO 31000 series of standards. AIROâ€™s core concepts and relations are illustrated below. The upper half shows the main concepts required for describing an AI System and the lower half of the figure represents essential concepts for expressing Risk}. The relation isRiskAssociatedWith links these two halves by connecting risk to either a system or a component of the system. ISO 31000 sees risk as being both an opportunity and a threat. However, in the context of the AI Act the concept of Risk, and therefore its Consequence and Impact refers to the risk of harm. To reflect this, AIRO only refers to risks in the context of harms. AIRO also distinguishes between consequence and impact to indicate consequence as direct outcomes which may or may not involve individuals, which can then lead to an impact (harm) to some subjects. Risks, consequences, and impacts can be addressed using Control that can relate to detection, mitigation and elimination. To further expand AIRO, the top-level concepts are populated by the instances obtained from the AI Act and ISO/IEC 22989. Then, the instances are classified using a bottom-up approach.
